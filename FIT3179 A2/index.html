World Air Quality: Update 2016

Data source: World Health Organization (WHO)

Why it is important? As air quality declines, the risk of stroke, lung cancer, and chronic and acute respiratory diseases, including asthma, increases for the people who live in them.

How we measure? The WHO air quality database compiles data on ground measurements of annual mean concentrations of particulate matter of a diameter equal or smaller than 2.5 Î¼m (PM2.5) which aim at representing an average for the urban or rural area as a whole, rather than for individual stations. Both groups of pollutants originate mainly from human activities related to fossil fuel combustion. The database is updated regularly every 2-3 years since 2011 and now covering 3000 cities in 103 countries.

The world's average PM10 levels by region range from 26 to 208 ug/m3, with a world's average of 71 ug/m3.







Looking back over these six years, what has happend to Air quality in different continent?

PM2.5 (Air quality) is more variable in Africa, Eastern mediterranean and South-East Asia, while it is more stable in the other continents;

PM2.5 is consistently highest in the Eastern Mediterranean (worst air quality), followed by the Africas and South-East Asia;

PM2.5 is consistently lowest in the Western Pacific (best air quality), followed by Europe, Americas.
